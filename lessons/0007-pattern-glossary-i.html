Design Taste/ Module B: Naming/ Lesson 07/ ~15 min

Pattern Glossary I: layout names

Your one win

By the end you can look at a page's skeleton and name it: six hero paradigms, the grid families (bento, masonry, split-screen, zigzag), and the ten composition anchors. Naming the layout is how you ask an AI for a structure instead of accepting the one it reaches for by reflex.

Style is what a page is made of. Pattern is how it is arranged. You have named ten style families; now name the arrangements. Every layout below has a real name in the corpus, and once you can see the silhouette you can call for it.

The thumbnails are deliberately stripped to wireframes: dark bars are headlines, thin bars are body text, the hatched panel is an image or asset, and the one red block is a call to action. A pattern is a silhouette. If you can recognize it in grayscale, you can name it on any real page.

Six hero paradigms

The first screen has six shapes

The hero is one moment, not a feature list. Almost every good one is a variant of these six. Name the one you want and you skip the centered-over-dark-mesh default.

H1Asymmetric Split

Text on one side, asset on the other, generous whitespace. The default workhorse hero for SaaS and premium consumer.

H2Editorial Manifesto

Huge type, no asset, almost a poster. For launches and editorial where the message itself is the design. The one time a centered hero is earned.

FORM

H3Video / Media Mask

Type cut out as a mask over a video or image background. Cinematic, brand-forward, heavy. Needs a real asset behind it.

typeMOVEStype

H4Kinetic-Type

Animated typography is the primary visual. The words perform. Portfolio and motion-studio energy; empty without real motion behind it.

H5Curtain-Reveal

The hero parts on scroll like a curtain to reveal what is behind it. A scroll-driven entrance, not a static layout.

H6Scroll-Pinned

The hero stays pinned in place while the rest of the content scrolls past behind it. Holds one message while the story moves.

Layout & grid families

Four ways to arrange the middle

Below the hero, sections belong to a family. Naming the family lets you break monotony on purpose instead of stacking the same row six times.

L1Bento Grid

Asymmetric tiles of mixed sizes, Apple Control Center style. Rhythm, not six identical cards.

L2Masonry Layout

A staggered grid with no fixed row height. Tiles pack by height, like a Pinterest wall. Good for galleries and mixed media.

↑ ↓

L3Split-Screen Scroll

Two halves that move in opposite directions as you scroll. One side holds while the other advances, or they counter-scroll.

L4Zigzag (alternating)

Image-left/text-right, then flipped. Readable, common, and capped: three in a row is banal. Break it by the third section.

Two rules that separate real bento from AI bento

The cell-count rule. A bento grid has exactly as many cells as you have content for. Three items, three cells. Five items, five cells. An empty tile in the middle or at the end means the grid was planned wrong. You reshape the grid; you never paste a blank to "balance" it.

The zigzag cap. The alternating image-and-text split is fine twice. The third one in a row is a pre-flight fail. Break the run with a full-width section, a bento, or a vertical stack. Below, the third row is flagged: that is the pattern going stale.

The ten composition anchors

Where the words sit in a section

Inside any one section, the text block can anchor in ten places. Reflex puts it left-of-image every time. Vary the anchor across a page and use at least three, and it stops reading as a template. This is the finest-grained layout vocabulary you have.

1. Centered statement

2. Top-left lead, support bottom-right

3. Bottom-left text over image

4. Bottom-right CTA cluster

5. Left-third caption, right visual

6. Right-third caption, left visual

7. Centered low, text in lower band

8. Off-grid editorial offset

9. Stacked center, ultra-minimal

10. Image-as-canvas, text in a safe area

Name the pattern

Both directions, mixed: sometimes you see a silhouette and name it, sometimes you get the name and pick the silhouette. That second direction is the one that proves you actually hold the vocabulary, not just recognize it.
