The Three Dials: Variance, Motion, Density

Your one win

By the end you can read any page as a triple like 7 / 6 / 4 and say what each number means. Spotting slop was Lesson 01. This is how you describe intent.

Designers do not argue about "make it nicer." They argue about specific, independent axes. This course uses three, borrowed straight from your skill corpus. Every page you will ever build sits somewhere on all three at once.

The three dials are independent: a page can be wildly asymmetric yet perfectly still, or dead-symmetric yet buzzing with motion. Learning to move them separately in your head is most of what "having an eye" means.

What each dial actually controls

Drag each slider to its extremes and watch which parts of the page respond. That mapping is the whole lesson.

Variance — symmetry to chaos

At low variance everything is centered and mirror-balanced. In the middle the hero splits and text left-aligns. At high variance the grid breaks: unequal columns, offset blocks, large empty zones. Most AI slop sits at low variance without meaning to, which is why Lesson 01's centered hero felt so default.

Motion — static to cinematic

Low is still: hover states only. The middle adds transitions and a gentle entrance fade (hit "Replay entrance"). High adds continuous, choreographed motion, and the corpus's rule kicks in: motion must be motivated, and it must respect prefers-reduced-motion. This page does: the infinite float only runs at high motion, and turns off entirely if your system asks for reduced motion.

Density — art gallery to cockpit

Low density is airy: huge whitespace, big type, generous cards. High density is a cockpit: tight padding, cards collapse into 1px divider rows, and numbers switch to a monospace font so columns of data line up. Watch the feature row transform as you push density past 7.

The baseline

The corpus ships a default of 8 / 6 / 4 — confident variance, moderate motion, fairly airy. It is a starting point, not a law. The design read (next lesson) is what moves you off the baseline.

Read the dials yourself

Five pages, each rendered at fixed settings. For each, read the layout, motion, and spacing, then enter your best guess for all three dials (1-10). You score a hit for any dial within +/-1 of the real value. Aim to feel the bands (low / mid / high) more than the exact number.
